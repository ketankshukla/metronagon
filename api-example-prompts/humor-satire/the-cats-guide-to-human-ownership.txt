A clever, deadpan book cover rendered in the precise, authoritative style of Victorian natural history illustration — the engraved, crosshatched, scientifically labeled tradition used to document exotic species in 19th-century zoological encyclopedias. The subject: a common domestic cat, rendered with the same reverence and scientific precision normally reserved for newly discovered jungle cats or rare predatory birds. The humor is in the TREATMENT — the cat is illustrated as the dominant, superior species, while a human (barely visible in the background) is depicted as the cat's domesticated servant animal.

THE MAIN ILLUSTRATION — THE CAT (center, 20-75% from top):

A single domestic cat — a well-fed tabby — rendered in a formal scientific specimen pose: standing in profile (the standard natural history convention), head turned slightly toward the viewer, tail held at an elegant angle. The cat is illustrated with the obsessive detail of a master natural history engraver:

THE RENDERING: Fine pen-and-ink crosshatching — the traditional engraving technique where tonal values are built from networks of fine parallel and crossing lines. The crosshatching follows the direction of the fur, creating the characteristic texture of animal illustration: dense, tight hatching on the dark tabby stripes, lighter, wider-spaced hatching on the paler areas, and areas of bare paper for the brightest highlights (the white chest, the eye highlights).

THE CAT'S EXPRESSION: Here the illustration departs from standard natural history: the cat has a distinctly SUPERIOR expression. The eyes are half-lidded with the particular disdain that only cats achieve. The mouth curves in what might be a smirk. The overall impression is of a being that is allowing itself to be illustrated as a favor to the artist.

THE POSE: While formally in the "specimen profile" position, the cat's body language radiates authority — the spine is straight, the head is held high, and one front paw is slightly raised as though about to issue a command or bat something off a table.

SCIENTIFIC ANNOTATIONS: Leader lines extend from various parts of the cat to small labels in precise italic serif lettering:
- From the eyes: "Oculi imperiosus — capable of communicating absolute contempt at distances up to 30 metres"
- From the claws: "Ungues retractilis — deployed at 3am on owner's face"
- From the tail: "Cauda expressiva — current position indicates mild tolerance"
- From the stomach: "Venter insatiabilis — demands feeding at all hours; food preferences change without notice"
- From the ears: "Aures selectivae — capable of hearing tin opener at 200m; deaf to own name at any range"

THE HUMAN (background, small, lower-right, 60-80% from top):

In the background, rendered at a MUCH SMALLER SCALE (the natural history convention where the main specimen is large and its habitat or associated species are small), a human figure — a person in casual clothes, hunched over, holding a food bowl and approaching the cat with a subservient posture. The human is labeled with a leader line: "Homo domesticus — common house-servant of F. catus. Trainable. Responds well to persistent vocalization at dawn."

The human is rendered with deliberately LESS detail than the cat — looser hatching, less precise anatomy, simpler features. The visual hierarchy is clear: the cat is the subject of scientific interest; the human is merely its environment.

The illustration is presented as a formal natural history plate:
- PLATE NUMBER: "PLATE LXII" in small serif at the top
- SPECIES NAME: Below the plate number, in large italic serif: "Felis catus domesticus supremus" — a deliberately inflated Latin binomial

- ARTIST CREDIT: "Del. et sculp. Sir Reginald Whiskers III, F.R.S., 1887" — a cat's name with academic credentials
