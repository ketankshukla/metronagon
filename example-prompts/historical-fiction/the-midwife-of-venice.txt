THE PORTRAIT — THE MIDWIFE (centered, 15-85% from top):

A woman in her 30s, shown from the waist up in three-quarter view — turned slightly to the right, her gaze meeting the viewer's with calm intelligence. She is not a noblewoman but a professional — and her portrait captures the quiet authority of someone who holds the power of life and death.

FACE: Rendered with the warm, luminous glazing technique of Venetian portraiture — multiple translucent layers of oil paint creating skin that seems lit from within. Her complexion is warm olive (#D0A880) with rose undertones visible on cheeks and lips. The modeling is soft — gradual transitions from highlight to shadow with no hard edges, the sfumato technique creating the impression of real, breathing flesh. Her features are specific and individual (not idealized): a strong nose, full lips, dark intelligent eyes with visible catchlights, and a few fine lines that speak of experience. Her dark hair is simply arranged beneath a white linen coif — the practical head covering of a working woman, its crisp white fabric creating the painting's brightest near-white value.

HANDS: Visible at the lower portion of the portrait — the hands are given particular attention, as they are the midwife's primary tools. The right hand holds a small sprig of RUE (Ruta graveolens) — the herb historically associated with midwifery, with its distinctive blue-green (#4A7A6A) palmate leaves and small yellow flowers. The left hand rests on a book — a medical text, its leather cover dark and worn. The hands are rendered with anatomical precision and the warm, living quality of Venetian flesh painting.

CLOTHING: She wears the practical dress of a Venetian professional woman:
- A fitted bodice in deep Venetian red (#6A1A1A) — the famous rosso Veneziano, a warm, brownish crimson characteristic of Venetian dyeing. The fabric is rendered with Titian's characteristic loose, sensual brushwork — the paint itself suggests the heavy, slightly textured surface of Venetian wool.
- White linen chemise visible at the neckline and sleeves — bright, crisp white (#F0E8E0) fabric rendered with tight, precise brushwork contrasting with the looser treatment of the red wool.
- A simple gold chain around her neck with a small pendant — a pelican-in-her-piety (a Christian symbol of self-sacrifice, associated with healers).

The background is NOT flat — it is painted with visible brushwork and tonal variation, warmer and slightly lighter behind the figure's lit side, cooler and darker on the shadow side. PAINTING TECHNIQUE — VENETIAN SCHOOL:

- WARM GLAZING: The entire painting glows with warm tones — even the shadows are warm (brown, olive, umber) rather than cool. This is achieved through the Venetian technique of building color in translucent glazes over a warm-toned ground.
- LOOSE BRUSHWORK: The brushwork is visible and expressive — particularly in the clothing and background, where individual brush strokes suggest texture and movement. The flesh areas are more smoothly blended.
- IMPASTO HIGHLIGHTS: The brightest highlights (the white coif, the catchlight in the eye, the gold chain) are rendered in thick, raised paint — the characteristic impasto technique of Venetian masters.
- WARM GROUND: A warm reddish-brown ground color shows through in places — particularly in the background and shadow areas — creating the unified warm tonality that is the Venetian signature.
- CANVAS TEXTURE: At high resolution, the weave of the canvas is faintly visible beneath the paint — the characteristic texture of Venetian stretched linen.
